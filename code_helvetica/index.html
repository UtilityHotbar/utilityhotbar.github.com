0 Introduction

Question: What is Code Helvetica?

Answer: Code Helvetica is a post-cyberpunk rules-light roleplaying game. You play as a team of misfits in a futuristic megapolis that each follow a Code, a set of personal ethics that defines you as a character and gives you access to unique abilities.

 

Question: How is Code Helvetica different to Shadowrun/other cyberpunk RPGs?

Answer: Code Helvetica is founded on these principles-

  1. Post-cyberpunk: The world is hard, the revolutionaries are flawed, the man is here for a reason.
  2. Human in the shell: It rains all the time, the pollution gets in your lungs, and the drugs send you on bad trips. No cyber-elves, thank you very much.
  3. High-risk, high-reward: One wrong move could put you in incredible danger- but getting it done looks amazing.

 

Question: How do I use this document?

Answer: This document contains the basic rules for Code Helvetica. A supplementary document gives more information on the setting of Code Helvetica and the various factions involved.

 

Question: How is this document formatted?

Answer: Key terms are highlighted. Verbs (actions) are emphasised.

 

1 The Gambit

The gambit is Code Helvetica's resolution method. When there is a conflict involving characters where the outcome is unclear, the GM will start a 60 second Decision Clock. This can be because the players want to accomplish something (crashing a corporate barricade) or because some danger is about to befall the players (A truck is spiralling towards their section of the highway).

 

1.1 Dares and Wagers

To use a gambit, the players first narrate the action they will take and the consequence they hope to cause - the dare. The GM then makes a counter-offer, listing the success conditions and possible negative consequences. The players can then limit or expand upon their action as they choose. Two or more players can participate in a gambit.

 

The currency with which a gambit is executed are known as Focus dice. These represent abstract mental effort, and all players involved in a gambit can choose how much to contribute from their stash such that they have a chance of meeting their success conditions. This is their wager. The wager is then rolled collectively.

 

On a success, half the Focus dice rounded down are returned to each player. On a failure, the consequences listed occur and no dice are returned.

 

Here is an example gambit:

Kyna has 5 Focus dice. Bryce has 3 Focus dice. They are currently riding a motorcycle, attempting to crash an armed corporate barricade.

KYNA: We've come up with a plan. I break through the barricade with my 'cycle, crashing into two guards while Bryce shoots to knock another one down.

GM: That's going to be tricky with your hit points. I think you'd need at least two 8s and one 7 to succeed. If you fail, you might crash into the ground and explode.

KYNA: Okay, I'll just focus on breaking through the barricade while avoiding fire then.

GM: That's going to be two 7s in total.

BRYCE: Sounds good to me- I'll wager 2 Focus.

KYNA: Since I've got more, I'll wager 3. Can I add my Vehicle Operation skill?

GM: Sure thing. That's two more dice.

Kyna and Bryce roll 7 dice in total, rolling 1, 2, 5, 7, 8, 3, and 1. They crash heroically through the barricade, and regain 1 Focus dice each.

Gambits are always player facing, meaning that the question of success or failure will always be in the player's hands.

If the player commits 0 Focus dice (unless a relevant skill is involved), the action automatically fails.

 

1.2 Adjudicating Gambits

To determine the difficulty and consequences for a gambit, follow this 3-step process.

  1. How many problems does it solve? -> Ask for this many results. (Extraordinary problems may require 2 successes to solve, 3 or more should be ruled as impossible)
  2. For each problem, how difficult is the problem? -> Use this to determine the number required per problem. (Code Helvetica uses a roll-over system, meaning you roll equal to or above the target number)
  3. For each problem, how bad are the consequences? -> Pick the worst consequence. (It's usually enough)

1.3 Tools and Bonuses

Appropriate tools can reduce the difficulty of a related task by up to -5 when used correctly with time to prepare. For example, using a grappling hook accurately might turn the third gambit to a difficulty 2 challenge, whereas using a grappling hook while dodging fire might reduce the fourth gambit to 4, 7 only. Tools may also mitigate consequences, for example slowed falling while using a grappling hook.

 

Situational bonuses can also increase or reduce the difficulty up to +/-3. For example, climbing a slick medium wall during heavy rain may require a 7 instead of a 5. If the final value is over 8, carry over the value minus 8 if it is greater than 1. For example, gambit 3 in a rainstorm might take 8, 2 instead of 7.

1.4 Using Skills

If the GM decides that a skill applies to a given gambit, Focus dice are added to the dice pool based on the skill value, either positive or negative. For example, a player with 2 ranks in a relevant skill adds 2 free Focus dice to the dice pool, where a player with -1 ranks must take away 1 Focus dice from the dice pool.

 

2 Character Creation

To create a character, first roll 7 + 1d6 to determine your hit points. Your maximum hit points = starting hit points + 5. All characters start with 10 Focus dice (they can have 15 dice maximum), and can regain them by resting (1d6 every 10 minutes rested) or by acting in accordance with their Code (see below). Each character also begins with 7 character points. Character points can be spent to buy skills, lifestyle, or reputation. Unspent character points can also be used to add Focus dice to any gambit at the rate of 1 dice per point.

2.1 Skills

Skills represent areas of specialty your character is good (or bad) at. Buying positive skill ranks requires increasing amounts of character points (1, 3, 5, 7 and so on), whereas buying negative skill ranks gives you 1 character point per rank. 2.3 Reputation

Reputation determines the access to resources and connections your character has. There are two types of reputation: Street Rep (Street Reputation)and Cit Rep (Citizen's Reputation). Street Rep measures your renown in the murky criminal underworld that lurks beneath the glamour of the corporate halls. Cit Rep measures your relationship with the authorities, corporations, and general forces of control within the city.

To buy from a vendor, roll under your rep with that faction, then subtract from your rep based on the value of that item: 0 for weak items or tools, 1 for basic items, 2 for dangerous items, and 3 for very dangerous items. If your rep goes below 0 with a faction, you have a debt (a favour you must repay).

To use your reputation, subtract the value of the effect from your reputation. For example, to call in a few street toughs, subtract 1 from your Street Rep.

NOTE: You can have ranks in both Street Rep and Cit Rep at the same time- two lives are more interesting than one, after all.

3 Codes

Your Code will be the defining feature of your character. Actions in line with your Code get +1 Focus, while actions not in line get -1 Focus. This game features three base codes: The titular Code Helvetica, the Rider's Oath, and the Operator's Handbook.

 

3.1 Code Helvetica

Those who follow the Code Helvetica are a secretive group of decentralised hackers united by their resistance to centralised authority. They are based chiefly in the digital domain of the Free Swiss Republic, a cyber-nation that affords its citizens with decentralised banking, communication, and data storage services all defined by the Citizen's Contract, guaranteeing the network a share of the profits from any job. The Code Helvetica consists of these central principles:

 

Backdoors over Frontdoors. Never do something loudly when you can do it quietly. In and out unnoticed is the true sign of mastery.

Silence over Violence. If bullets are flying, something's gone wrong. Violence is the last-ditch solution, a sign of losing control.

Mind over Matter. The flesh is weak. The mind is supreme.

 

Equipment: All followers of Code Helvetica are given a cyber-deck. While "jacked in" to the deck, the user gains 1 rank in all digital skills automatically. If the deck can be used to interface with a smart object, the user can attempt to hack that smart object with +2 Focus dice. Decide on the details of your deck: Colour (primary and any accents) and design or decals (wires, plastic frame, head-frame/helmet, stickers).

 

Focus: Code Helvetica characters regain 1d10 Focus by completing a major hack or digital challenge.

 

3.2 Rider's Oath

The Rider's Oath binds together a loose collection of young, often violent street gangs known alternately as mat rempit or bosozoku. They are defined by their vehicles, lovingly polished and customised, which they use to enact brief, furious sequences of action. Like the members of Code Helvetica, their physical locations are amorphous, with many spending their days as teenagers in school or salarymen trapped in dead-end jobs. However, unlike the Code, they work almost entirely within what the cyber-thief diminutively calls "meatspace"- nothing quite beats the wind in your hair, after all. The Rider's Oath (translated from modern youth vernacular) reads:

 

Move fast, break things. The night is young. Speed is of the utmost essence. Planning and computers are for losers.

Style first. If there's a way forward that's cool, stylish, and powerful, do it. All other concerns are secondary.

Loyalty is everything. Stick with your brothers. The streets are harsh places, so watch each other's back.

 

Equipment: Those who swear the oath of the rider gain a customised vehicle, usually a bike, known as a ride. Being rammed by a ride is dangerous at normal speed and very dangerous at high speed. Decide on the details of your ride: Colour (primary and any accents) and finish or decals (chrome, matte, bakelite, stickers).

 

Focus: Riders regain 1d10 Focus by pausing after completing a thrilling stunt.

 

3.3 Operator's Handbook

An Operator is a corporate asset. They usually operate in structured teams based on the mission they set out to accomplish, and can be either convenient or expendable depending on the nature of the mission. They will usually be given a (bugged) base of operations they must report to, using whatever tools they are given or have available on-site to finish a wide variety of tasks. From garden variety blackmail to assassinations from atop skyscrapers in the dead of night, operators do it all. What do their handbooks say?

 

Minimise liability. A crashed car is a liability HQ has to clean/cover up. A witness is a liability HQ has to clean/cover up.

Always be on guard. You never know who's going to backstab you next, and its probably the person in charge of giving you a pay cheque.

Plan first, act second. There are old operators, there are bold operators, but there are no old and bold operators.

 

Equipment: Operators usually have access to better gear than everyone else. Select one piece of gear from the Equipment section assuming you put 5 character points in.

 

Focus: Operator characters regain 1d10 Focus by executing a plan flawlessly or completing an objective.

 

3.4 Agent's Field Guide

An agent is a government asset. The government still exists, albeit in a lesser form- In its hands are large budgets that always seem to disappear into the hands of defence and intelligence contractors, leaving pittances for social programs. An agent spends as much time fighting the massive bureaucracy that forms the crumbling morass of government as working for it, especially when fickle or short-minded leaders are elected by the corporations- sorry, by the people influenced by corporations.

 

Follow the rules. You aren't savages or operatives. The laws exist for people like you.

Document everything. Evidence is the most important weapon you can have, especially in the bureaucracy.

Improvise. You won't have the best equipment or tricks. Use what you have to get an edge in a cold world.

Equipment: Agents get a set of camera shades, capable of recording up to 10 hours of video continuously. They also look intimidating, and are useful for stymying facial-recognition software built to recognise eyes.

 

Focus: Agents regain 1d10 focus after successfully defusing a difficult situation or overcoming a superior (corporate) enemy.
