> Introduction

Question: What is Code Helvetica?

Answer: Code Helvetica is a post-cyberpunk rules-light roleplaying game. You play as a team of misfits in a futuristic megapolis that each follow a Code, a set of personal ethics that defines you as a character and gives you access to unique abilities.

 

Question: How is Code Helvetica different to Shadowrun/other cyberpunk RPGs?

Answer: Code Helvetica is founded on these principles-

  1. Post-cyberpunk: The world is hard, the revolutionaries are flawed, the man is here for a reason.
  2. Human in the shell: It rains all the time, the pollution gets in your lungs, and the drugs send you on bad trips. No cyber-elves, thank you very much.
  3. High-risk, high-reward: One wrong move could put you in incredible danger- but getting it done looks amazing.

 

Question: How do I use this document?

Answer: This document contains the basic rules for Code Helvetica and gives you a starter adventure, The Oracular Job. A supplementary document gives more information on the setting of Code Helvetica and the various factions involved.

 

Question: How is this document formatted?

Answer: Key terms are highlighted. Verbs (actions) are emphasised.

 

> The Gambit

Code Helvetica functions, at its heart, like most other role-playing games. Players narrate their actions, the GM narrates the results and any developments, and when an action is selected that carries a risk of consequences or failure a dice-based mechanic is used to determine the outcome. In this game, however, the mechanic is slightly different from the standard skill check.

 

In Code Helvetica a "check" is known as a gambit. At a key decision point, the GM will start a 60-second timer for the players to decide on their next course of action. A gambit has two core components: the dare and the wager. To begin, a player narrates the action they will take and the consequence they will hope to cause- the dare. The GM makes a counter-offer, listing the success conditions and possible negative consequences if the character fails. The player can then limit or expand upon their action as they choose.

 

Once the terms of the gambit have been decided upon, the player then decides how much dice (generally use d8s) to wager. Each player has a pool of Focus dice representing their mental ability to concentrate on any situation. The Focus dice chosen are then rolled, with the highest number representing the result. On a success, half the Focus dice rounded down are returned. On a failure, the consequences are suffered and no dice are returned.

Kyna is a player with 5 d8 Focus dice. She is currently riding a motorcycle, attempting to crash an armed corporate barricade.

KYNA: I break through the barricade with my 'cycle, crashing into two guards while shooting to knock another one down.

GM: That's going to be tricky with your low hit points. I think you'd need at least two 8s to succeed, and if you fail you might crash into the ground and explode.

KYNA: Okay, I'll just focus on breaking through the barricade while avoiding fire then.

GM: I think that needs a 7 or higher. If you fail, the guards get a free shot at you.

KYNA: Sounds good to me. I wager 3 of my Focus dice.

Kyna rolls a 3, 5, and an 8. She succeeds at her action, heroically breaking through the defences, and regains 1 Focus die. She now has 3 Focus dice to spend.

Alternatively, sometimes the GM may narrate an impending threat and ask the players to describe their response. This is known as a saving gambit. Dice rolls in Code Helvetica are always player-facing, meaning that the question of success or failure will always be in the player's hands.

If the player commits 0 Focus dice (Unless they have a Strength), the action automatically fails. Players may also have Strengths and Weaknesses. A Strength grants 1 extra Focus die for free per level and a Weakness removes 1 Focus die before rolling per level. Multiple Strengths and Weaknesses can overlap.

 

> Creating a Character

A baseline character in Code Helvetica has 2d6 hit points (alternatively, roll 3d6, dropping the lowest and rerolling any 1s) and 10 Focus Dice. The Physical Endurance Strength grants an extra 1d4 hit points per rank. All characters regain Focus Dice by resting for up to 30 minutes (1d6 per 10 minutes rested), and each Code has their own unique way of regenerating Focus (see below).

$ Character points

Characters also begin with 7 character points. Each character point can be used to buy a Strength. If characters select a Weakness, they gain 1 character point. Buying more than 1 rank in a Strength requires 2 more character points per rank (1, 3, 5, 7 etc.). Selecting more than 1 rank of a weakness still only gives 1 point per rank. $ Equipment and Reputation

In addition to strengths and weaknesses, characters can also use character points to purchase Equipment or gain Reputation. Equipment represents physical tools the characters buy to assist them before a mission, while Reputation is an abstract score that tracks a characters degree of renown, helping facilitate purchases or open doors during a mission. A maximum of 5 points can be invested into either category.

===Equipment===

To determine the starting equipment for each character, decide how many character points you wish to invest, then roll 1d6 twice to determine equipment, rerolling doubles:

Everyone with more than 2 character points invested starts with a headset, an always-on device that can transmit information from the 'brane- the internet- into your brain directly.

===Reputation===

There are two types of Reputation - Street Reputation (Street Rep) and Citizen's Reputation (Cit Rep). Street Rep measures your renown in the murky criminal underworld that lurks beneath the glamour of the corporate halls. Cit Rep measures your relationship with the authorities, corporations, and general forces of control within the city.

NOTE: You can have ranks in both Street Rep and Cit Rep at the same time- two lives are more interesting than one, after all.

How to Use: To use reputation, subtract the effect you want from your Rep. For example, if you want to get a few street toughs on your side, "cash in" 1 Street Rep. To see whether you can buy something, roll 1d6- If you roll under your Rep with that vendor (Street Rep for black market, Cit Rep for corporate stores), you can buy it. Weak items cost 1 rep, basic items 2, and so on.

> Selecting a Code

After creating a basic character, you will select a Code. Aside from your Skills, Equipment, and Reputation, your Code will be the central feature that defines your character. Actions in line with your Code will always be Strengths, and actions violating your Code will always be Weaknesses. This game features three base codes: The titular Code Helvetica, the Rider's Oath, and the Operator's Handbook.

$ Code Helvetica

Those who follow the Code Helvetica are a secretive group of decentralised hackers and crackers (hackers make things, crackers break into things). They are based chiefly in the digital domain of the Free Swiss Republic, a cyber-nation that affords its citizens with decentralised banking, communication, and data storage services all guaranteed by the Citizen's Contract, guaranteeing the network a share of the profits from any job. The Code Helvetica consists of these central principles:

 

Backdoors over Frontdoors. Never do something loudly when you can do it quietly. In and out unnoticed is the true sign of mastery.

Silence over Violence. If bullets are flying, something's gone wrong. Violence is the last-ditch solution, a sign of losing control.

Mind over Matter. The flesh is weak. The mind is supreme.

 

Equipment: All followers of Code Helvetica are given a cyber-deck. While "jacked in" to the deck, the user gains 1 rank in all digital skills automatically. If the deck can be used to interface with a smart object, the user can attempt to hijack that smart object with +1 Focus dice. Decide on the details of your deck: Colour (primary and any accents) and design or decals (wires, plastic frame, head-frame/helmet, stickers).

 

Focus: Code Helvetica characters regain Focus by completing a major hack or digital challenge.

 

$ Rider's Oath

The Rider's Oath binds together a loose collection of young, often violent street gangs known alternately as bosozoku. They are defined by their vehicles, lovingly polished and customised, which they use to enact brief, furious sequences of action. Like the members of Code Helvetica, their physical locations are amorphous, with many spending their days as teenagers in school or salarymen trapped in dead-end jobs. However, unlike the Code, they work almost entirely within what the cyber-thief diminutively calls "meatspace"- nothing quite beats the wind in your hair, after all. The Rider's Oath (translated from the modern youth vernacular) reads:

 

Move fast, break things. The night is young. Speed is of the utmost essence. Planning and computers are for losers.

Style first. If there's a way forward that's cool, stylish, and powerful, do it. All other concerns are secondary.

Loyalty is everything. Stick with your brothers. The streets are harsh places, so watch each other's back.

 

Equipment: Those who swear the oath of the rider gain a customised vehicle, usually a bike, known as a ride. Being rammed by a ride is dangerous at normal speed and very dangerous at high speed. Decide on the details of your ride: Colour (primary and any accents) and finish or decals (chrome, matte, bakelite, stickers).

 

Focus: Rider's Oath characters regain Focus by pausing after completing a thrilling stunt.

 

$ Operator's Handbook

An Operator is a corporate or government asset. They usually operate in structured teams based on the mission they set out to accomplish, and can be either invaluable or expendable depending on the nature of the mission. They will usually be given a (bugged) base of operations they must report to, using whatever tools they are given or have available on-site to finish a wide variety of tasks. From garden variety blackmail to assassinations from atop skyscrapers in the dead of night, operators do it all. What do their handbooks say?

 

Minimise liability. A crashed car is a liability HQ has to clean/cover up. A witness is a liability HQ has to clean/cover up.

Always be on guard. You never know who's going to backstab you next, and its probably the person in charge of giving you a pay cheque.

Plan first, act second. There are old operators, there are bold operators, but there are no old and bold operators.

 

Equipment: Operators (unless they are government-run) usually have access to better gear than everyone else. Select one piece of gear from the Equipment section assuming you put 5 character points in.

 

Focus: Operator characters regain Focus by executing a plan flawlessly or completing an objective.

> GM Guidance

Running a game is hard. Here are some ways to lighten the burden, perhaps slightly.

$ Arbitrating Gambits

To determine the difficulty and consequences for a gambit, follow this 3-step process.

  1. How many problems does it solve? -> Ask for this many results. (Extraordinary problems may require 2 successes to solve, any more and you should ask a player to split up their gambit so as not to steal the show)
  2. For each problem, how difficult is the problem? -> Use this to determine the number required per problem. (1 = certain success, 8 = almost impossible)
  3. For each problem, how bad are the consequences? -> Pick the worst consequence. (It's usually enough)

$ Creating opposition

Corps or groups have a code of their own, which they obey as an organisation. Following these guidelines can make for a more realistic and enjoyable play experience. The Organisational Guides are:

 

Faces, not spaces. Every organisation should have recognisable spokespeople or agents your characters interact with. People enjoy working with (or against!) people. These faces, however, are temporary and ultimately replaceable- the death of the CEO doesn't mean the end for real life megacorps, so why should it for fictional ones?

Omnipresent but not omnipotent. A corporation should be far-reaching, yes- but never so powerful that resistance is always futile. Always find ways to remind your players of the organisation's grasp, but never use it to intentionally railroad your players.

The system is human. No matter how sophisticated the AIs or computer networks in control, ultimately they are still monitored by people (unless your story is about the rise of AI corporations!). People make mistakes. People can be bribed or... persuaded. People are the weak link in any system.
